Wave Release Coordination under Vertical Resource Constraints in Multi-Storey AMR Warehouses

Shiyue Hu  ·  (co-authors TBD)
Draft v0.1, April 2026

Abstract

In multi-storey fulfilment warehouses, autonomous mobile robots (AMRs) are confined to floors and share a small number of freight elevators that connect the levels. Because elevator capacity is the system's binding constraint, the upstream decision of which orders to release together (the wave composition) shapes how hard every downstream AMR–elevator dispatch problem becomes. The joint study of wave composition with elevator capacity coupling in the multi-storey AMR warehouse literature remains, to our knowledge, an open research direction. We formalise this gap as the wave release coordination problem under vertical resource constraints, a two-stage scheduler that decouples tactical wave composition from operational AMR–elevator dispatch, and develop two analytical tools on this scaffold. The first is a Bound-and-Gap decomposition theorem applied to a three-dimensional structured representation $\Phi = (C, I, T)$ of wave composition, namely vertical concentration, directional imbalance, and temporal clustering; the theorem splits the value of wave-structure information into two additive components. The second is a Model-Dominance Hedge Rule, a minimax-collapse theorem under per-wave stochastic dominance. Together, these tools deliver three operational outcomes: (i) a per-regime diagnostic of whether wave design, additional elevator capacity, or neither merits investment; (ii) a one-line closed-form dispatch policy with provably bounded worst-case loss, requiring no online model selection; and (iii) a tactical-operational substitutability map, confirmed by a pre-registered simulation experiment, that prospectively classifies which operating regimes benefit from tactical wave design.

1. Introduction

E-commerce growth and the shift to next-day and same-day fulfilment have pulled distribution centres closer to dense urban customers, where land is scarce and horizontal expansion is bounded by zoning and price. Operators increasingly resort to multi-storey fulfilment buildings, stacking warehouse floors vertically inside a constrained urban footprint to obtain the throughput a flat warehouse would deliver only at much greater distance from customers. These buildings are typically staffed by autonomous mobile robots (AMRs) that handle horizontal travel within each floor, while a small number of freight elevators connect the levels and carry every floor-to-floor transfer for the entire fleet. The new bottleneck is no longer floor space or robot count: it is elevator capacity, and dozens of AMRs may sit idle waiting for a handful of elevator trips.

Operators manage this congestion by releasing orders in waves, short release windows during which a chosen subset of orders is dispatched together. The composition of each wave (which orders to bundle) directly determines how many vertical transitions the elevators must serve, in which directions, and with what temporal stagger; it therefore sets the difficulty of the downstream dispatch problem before any robot moves. In current warehouse practice, wave composition is set heuristically by destination floor, carrier cutoff, or arrival order, because no available framework ties this tactical decision to its operational consequences under shared-elevator coupling. Yet the wave layer is the cheapest place to relieve a vertical bottleneck: re-batching already-released orders changes neither the floor layout nor the fleet size. Despite this leverage, these four elements (wave composition, multi-storey deployment, flexible AMR fleets, and shared-elevator capacity) have so far been studied separately; their joint coupling defines a problem class that has not yet been formalised.

Prior work has addressed parts of this coupling separately: order batching in vertical lift modules [Lenoble et al., 2018], multi-agent robot–lift scheduling [Chakravarty et al., 2025], tier-captive multi-storey shuttles [Wu et al., 2024; Keung, 2023], and planar AMR fulfilment treating order-grouping or wave-cardinality decisions [Qin et al., 2024; Scholz et al., 2017; Žulj et al., 2022]. Two methodological lines also bear on our framing: robust scheduling under model uncertainty [Wiesemann et al., 2013; Lu and Shen, 2021] and prediction-to-decision regret [Elmachtoub and Grigas, 2022; Vera et al., 2021], from both of which our methodology departs in well-defined ways. A detailed thread-by-thread comparison appears in our Related Work section.

Beyond this prior work, two empirical observations from a regime sweep across representative operating conditions further shape the methodology, and each maps directly to one of the tools we build. First, wave structure carries bounded but consistent value: a structured-feature wave choice beats a random wave in every regime tested, yet the lift sits well below an oracle's. This finding is what drives our Bound-and-Gap framework, a decomposition theorem that names the gap between informed policy and oracle and splits it into two interpretable components, producing a per-regime diagnostic of where wave-design effort actually pays off. Second, the choice of elevator model can flip structural conclusions: re-running identical waves under throughput aggregation versus true co-occupancy batching can reverse the sign of a key feature coefficient, yet the underlying per-wave dominance ordering between the two models is highly stable. This stability is what lets us collapse the minimax decision over competing elevator abstractions into a one-line policy with bounded worst-case loss, without ever needing to choose the “right” model online.

These observations motivate three contributions, each pairing a methodological action with its operational outcome.

(C1) Problem formulation. We formalise the wave release coordination problem under vertical resource constraints as a two-stage scheduler that decouples tactical wave composition from operational AMR–elevator execution under a hard per-trip elevator capacity. The outcome is the first explicit specification of the joint problem class: wave composition, multi-storey deployment, flexible AMR fleets, and shared-elevator capacity treated as one coupled system rather than five separate strands of literature.

(C2) Methodology. We develop three coordinated tools, each a method paired with the operational lever it produces. First, we decompose wave composition along three physically interpretable axes (vertical concentration, directional imbalance, and temporal clustering), giving each feature a per-unit operational meaning that operators can act on. Second, we prove a Bound-and-Gap decomposition theorem that splits the value of wave-structure information into two non-negative components, yielding a per-regime diagnostic that tells operators whether to invest in wave design, in additional elevator capacity, or in neither. Third, we derive a Model-Dominance Hedge Rule, a minimax-collapse theorem under per-wave stochastic dominance, that reduces the decision over competing elevator abstractions to a one-line closed-form rule, bounding worst-case loss without requiring online model selection.

(C3) Empirical insights. Three findings emerge from our experiments, each tying one tool to a measurable operational gain. First, applying the Bound-and-Gap diagnostic prospectively classifies the operating regimes in which destination-clustered batching outperforms first-come-first-served dispatch, delivering an in-advance signal of where tactical wave design will pay off rather than a retrospective verdict. Second, the Hedge Rule's underlying dominance condition holds robustly across elevator capacities beyond those for which the rule was originally derived, extending the rule's reach without re-derivation. Third, we map the substitutability between tactical wave design and operational dispatch as a managerial diagram, producing a tool new to multi-storey AMR warehousing that tells operators which lever matters in which regime.

The remainder of the paper develops C1 (§4), C2 (§5), and C3 (§6), discusses managerial implications (§7), states limitations (§8), and concludes (§9).
